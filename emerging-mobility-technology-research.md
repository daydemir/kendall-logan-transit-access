# Emerging Mobility Technology Research: Kendall-Logan Airport Access

## Executive Summary

This research evaluates emerging mobility technologies for Kendall-Logan airport access, categorized by realistic implementation timelines:

**Short-term (0-5 years, 2025-2030):**
- Electric shuttle buses (immediate)
- Transit Signal Priority (TSP) systems (1-2 years)
- Microtransit/on-demand services (1-3 years)
- MaaS platform integration (2-4 years)

**Medium-term (5-15 years, 2030-2040):**
- Autonomous vehicle shuttles (5-10 years)
- Connected vehicle (V2X) corridors (5-8 years)
- eVTOL air taxis (8-15 years, limited scale)

**Key Finding:** Focus on proven electric vehicle and smart corridor technologies in the near term, while monitoring autonomous vehicle regulatory developments for medium-term implementation.

## 1. Autonomous Vehicle Shuttles

### Current State of Technology

Autonomous shuttle technology exists but remains largely restricted to controlled environments. As of 2024-2025:

- **Level 4 autonomy** (full autonomy with trained safety driver on board) is the current standard
- Typical operating speeds: 12-15 mph with maximum speeds of 25 mph
- Most deployments require geo-fencing, constant remote monitoring, and safety attendants
- Industry rollout remains "largely restricted to controlled environments such as airports"

### Airport Pilot Programs

**Dallas Fort Worth International Airport (DFW):**
- Began AV journey in 2019 with 15-person autonomous shuttle trials
- AV Landscape Research project (2023-2024) assessing market for autonomous people movement vehicles (APMs) and autonomous ground vehicles (AGVs)
- Evaluating companies for potential procurement and technology to retrofit existing vehicles

**Columbus, Ohio:**
- Linden LEAP pilot in South Linden neighborhood (1-year program)
- Level 4 AV with operator on board for safety override
- Programs transferred to Central Ohio Regional Transit Authority (COTA) for permanent operation

**Industry Developments (2025-2026):**
- Lyft partnering with Benteler to launch autonomous shuttle services starting late 2026
- ZF developing Level 4 shuttle with partner Beep, pilot programs scheduled for 2025
- EasyMile and NAVYA operating autonomous shuttles in 300+ locations across 30+ countries

### Massachusetts Regulatory Environment

**Current Testing Framework:**
- Executive Order 572 (signed October 2016) established Autonomous Vehicle Working Group
- MassDOT designated as lead agency for AV testing approval on public roads
- Currently only Motional permitted to test ADS-equipped vehicles in Boston and designated roadways
- **All testing requires a driver in the vehicle**

**Application Requirements:**
- Detailed 9-section application process
- Documentation of prior testing experience, safety assessment, training protocols
- First responder interaction plan
- Driver information, vehicle specifications, proof of insurance

**Pending Legislation:**
- Bill S.2351 under consideration includes:
  - Data privacy and security requirements
  - Passenger-accessible emergency stop mechanism (not relying on wireless connectivity)
  - Manufacturer/operator certification by Registrar for Level 3+ vehicles

**National Context:**
- 25 states introduced 67 new AV bills in early 2025
- Arizona, Louisiana, Montana, Nevada, and D.C. enacted AV legislation in 2024-2025
- NHTSA introduced AV STEP program (January 2025) establishing framework for reviewing and overseeing vehicles with automated driving systems

### Operating Costs

**Pilot Program Costs:**
- Denver self-driving shuttle pilot: $150,000
- Arlington, Texas (3 shuttles, 1 year): $435,000 (city paid 20%, federal grant 80%)
- Contra Costa County, CA (2-year pilot): $5 million (half private investment, half grants)
- Leasing costs: Well over $100,000 per vehicle per year

**Operating Cost Projections:**
- Current: $0.66-$1.00 per mile
- 2025 projection: $0.51 per mile
- 2030 projection: $0.25-$0.33 per mile
- Long-term (2035): $0.30-$0.50 per mile

**Key Cost Factors:**
- Labor currently makes up 70%+ of ride-hailing costs
- Battery replacement every 100,000 miles: $3,000-$15,000 (3-10Â¢ per mile)
- High cleaning, maintenance, and vandalism repair costs expected
- AV technology hardware: Currently $100,000 per vehicle, projected to drop to ~$3,000 by 2035

### Realistic Timeline for Kendall-Logan

**2025-2027: Not Feasible**
- Massachusetts regulatory framework still developing
- Limited to single permitted operator (Motional)
- Requires driver in vehicle
- Operating speeds (12-15 mph) too slow for Kendall-Logan distance

**2027-2030: Possible Pilot**
- If S.2351 passes and certification process established
- Potential small-scale pilot on dedicated route
- Would still require safety attendant
- Cost: $300,000-$500,000 for 1-year pilot

**2030-2035: More Realistic Deployment**
- Regulatory framework mature
- Operating costs competitive ($0.30-$0.50/mile)
- Technology proven in multiple airport environments
- Could operate without safety attendant

### Recommendations

**Near-term (2025-2027):** Do not pursue AV shuttle. Focus on electric conventional shuttles with smart corridor technology.

**Medium-term (2027-2030):** Monitor regulatory developments. Consider joining multi-stakeholder pilot if MassDOT establishes AV testing corridor.

**Long-term (2030+):** Re-evaluate for dedicated Kendall-Logan autonomous shuttle service.

## 2. Autonomous Air Mobility / eVTOL

### Industry Status

**Leading Companies:**

**Joby Aviation:**
- S4 eVTOL expected certification for commercial operations in 2025
- Planning air taxi service in Los Angeles and New York City starting 2025
- August 2025: Completed first flight between two U.S. airports (Marina to Monterey, ~10nm in 12 minutes)
- New York demonstration flights connecting Manhattan heliport to JFK and Newark airports
- John Wayne Airport has Joby-branded charging unit installed

**Archer Aviation:**
- "Hard core in certification phase" as of 2024
- Expected FAA certification end of 2024, deliveries beginning 2025
- August 2025: Completed 55-mile piloted flight (only 3 flights in July - slow pace)
- **Now targeting passenger service beginning 2026** (delayed from 2025)
- Chicago's Vertiport Chicago earmarked for O'Hare shuttle service

### Vertiport Infrastructure

**Cities with Multi-Site Vertiport Plans:**
- Dallas-Fort Worth
- Miami  
- Los Angeles
- New York
- Orlando (targeting 2028 go-live at airport)

**FAA Guidelines:**
- Published late 2024
- Standardize layout, safety features, site selection
- Clearing regulatory hurdles for expansion

**Infrastructure Examples:**
- Downtown Manhattan Heliport rebranded "Downtown Skyport" (April 2025) with eVTOL upgrades
- John Wayne Airport: $100M FBO redevelopment includes Joby charging unit

### Boston-Area Developments

**Key Finding: No Boston-area vertiport proposals found in research.**

Boston not mentioned in any eVTOL deployment plans or vertiport infrastructure announcements as of 2024-2025.

### Operating Economics

**Initial Fare Costs:**
- $100-$300 per trip initially
- Example comparison: $150-$200 for 15-minute eVTOL flight vs. $40-$80 for 45-minute taxi

**Capital Costs:**
- Aircraft: ~$5 million
- Battery replacement over 20-year life: $13 million (~$1M/year, $500/hour)
- Per-passenger capital cost (4 passengers, 75% load factor, 20-25 min flight): ~$70

**Operating Costs:**
- Full operating costs: ~$200 per passenger for short flight
- Operating costs: $250-$400 per hour
- Daily operating costs: $1,000-$2,000 for 4-5 flight hours

**Per-Mile Economics:**
- Joby estimate: $3.80 per mile (25-mile trip)
- Lilium estimate: $4.40 per mile (Manhattan to JFK at $70 total)
- Comparison: Helicopter trips currently $9/mile

**Cost Projections:**
- L.E.K. Consulting: eVTOL costs per passenger-km will decrease by 2/3 between 2025-2040
- Major cost components: Pilot salaries, energy costs, maintenance/repair/overhaul

**Economic Constraints:**
- High battery replacement costs are "major gotcha"
- Capacity limited to 2-5 passengers per trip
- Profitability dependent on automation and economies of scale

### Market Outlook

**Timeline:**
- Commercial services may commence in next 2-3 years
- **Will not reach scale until mid-2030s**
- Unlikely to become mass-market mode of travel
- Target market: Business and mid-to-high income individuals, expedited cargo

### Kendall Rooftop Vertiport Analysis

**Technical Considerations:**
- MIT and Kendall Square buildings could theoretically support rooftop vertiports
- Would need structural assessment, FAA approval, community engagement
- Noise concerns significant in dense urban environment

**Regulatory Hurdles:**
- No vertiport infrastructure in Massachusetts
- Would need to establish first in region
- Competing with other cities already advancing plans

**Economic Reality:**
- At $150-$300 per trip, limited market for Kendall-Logan connection
- Alternative: Logan rooftop to downtown Manhattan more economically viable use case

### Realistic Timeline for Kendall-Logan

**2025-2030: Not Feasible**
- No Boston-area vertiport infrastructure
- No operator interest announced
- Limited certification progress
- Operating economics don't support short 2-mile route

**2030-2035: Highly Unlikely**
- Even optimistic projections show limited scale by mid-2030s
- Boston not in first wave of deployments
- Cost per passenger ($200+) far exceeds value for 2-mile trip

**2035+: Possible But Impractical**
- Technology mature but economics still challenged
- Better use cases: Logan to downtown Boston, Logan to Worcester/Providence
- Kendall-Logan too short a distance to justify eVTOL

### Recommendations

**Do not pursue eVTOL for Kendall-Logan connection.** Route too short, no supporting infrastructure, no operator interest, economics unfavorable. Boston may see eVTOL service for longer routes (Logan-downtown, regional connections) but not for Kendall Square first/last mile.

## 3. Microtransit / On-Demand Services

### Successful Models

**Via Transit:**
- Operating successful partnerships with Worcester Regional Transit Authority (WRTA) in Massachusetts
- WRTA received $459,430 in discretionary MassDOT funding for Via partnership pilot
- Global operations in multiple cities

**MBTA On-Demand Paratransit:**
- Fall 2016: Began on-demand paratransit pilot with Uber/Lyft for 400 registered users
- Rider pays first $2, MBTA subsidizes up to $40 (rider responsible for amounts over $42)
- **Results:** 
  - Ridership increased from 10 to 13 trips per person per month
  - Cost decreased from $30/trip to $9/trip
  - Program extended to all registered paratransit users
- December 2024: New contract with Spare for paratransit technology and services

**Kansas City Area Transit Authority (KCATA):**
- Relaunched program after unsuccessful 2016 pilot
- Micro-transit ridership now 15x higher than 2016 pilot
- Schedules/routes adjust based on demand
- Wait times under 15 minutes

### The Bridj Failure: Critical Lessons

**Background:**
- Founded 2014, launched in Boston, Austin, Washington DC
- Used data (census, Facebook check-ins) to create dynamic transportation routes
- "Luxury bus service" targeting underserved areas
- Ceased operations April 2017

**Business Model Issues:**
- **Capital-intensive:** Owned and operated own vans, employees (not contractor drivers like Uber/Lyft)
- **Pricing:** Charged ~$5 per seat (2x MBTA bus fare) - not enough to cover overhead
- **High operating costs:** No public subsidies in Boston operations

**Operational Failures:**

**Kansas City Pilot ($1.3M, 1 year):**
- Served only 1,480 people in full year
- Six months in: Fewer than 600 rides (vs. 200/day projected)
- **Cost per ride: ~$1,000** (with public subsidy)
- Users: Younger and wealthier than typical KC resident
- Feedback: Service not robust enough to be useful

**Product Problems:**
- "Fancy bus stuck in traffic with everyone else" - Former MA Transportation Secretary Jim Aloisi
- Vast gulf between algorithmic promise and reality
- Fixed-route service with smartphone app, despite "on-demand" branding
- Failed to fulfill core promise of mobility transformation

**Downfall:**
- Attempted acquisition by major car company (rumored Toyota) fell through
- Without acquisition, business model unsustainable
- Acquired by Australia's Transit Systems, re-launched in Australia/Singapore

**Key Lessons:**
1. **On-demand availability and reliability central to microtransit value** - must be core from day one
2. **Public transit requires subsidy** - fares cannot cover all costs; capital-intensive private models fail
3. **Labor costs dominant** - owning vehicles and employing drivers dramatically increases overhead
4. **Service must be robust** - limited hours/routes make it "not useful enough"
5. **Algorithmic optimization alone insufficient** - still stuck in traffic, real-world constraints apply

### Massachusetts Regional Transit Authority (RTA) Microtransit

**Starting 2019, several RTAs piloted microtransit:**
- CATA On Demand
- CATCH Connect
- FRTA Access
- GATRA GO
- SmartDART
- Via WRTA (Worcester)

MBTA and 15 RTAs provide fixed-route and paratransit service across Massachusetts.

### Airport Last-Mile Applicability

**Successful Airport Microtransit Characteristics:**
- Dedicated, predictable service hours matching flight schedules
- Clear origin/destination pairs (airport terminals to specific Kendall locations)
- Integration with flight tracking for demand prediction
- Premium pricing justifiable for airport convenience
- Partnership with public transit agency or airport authority for subsidy/support

**Risks Based on Bridj Lessons:**
- Cannot be "stuck in traffic" - needs dedicated lanes or TSP
- Must have sufficient service frequency to be reliable
- Requires public subsidy if operated as public benefit
- Technology alone won't overcome fundamental constraints

### Realistic Timeline for Kendall-Logan

**2025-2026: Possible Pilot (1-2 years)**
- Partner with established provider (Via, Spare, not new startup)
- Leverage MBTA/Massport relationship
- Seek MassDOT discretionary funding (similar to WRTA's $459K)
- Start with limited hours (peak commute, major flight banks)
- Integrate with existing transit (not replacement)

**Cost Estimates:**
- Via/Spare partnership: $300,000-$500,000 for 1-year pilot
- Subsidy per ride: Target $5-$10 (not Bridj's $1,000)
- Fare: $8-$12 per trip (competitive with TNC, premium over transit)

**Success Factors:**
- Wait times <10 minutes
- Service hours 5am-11pm minimum
- Integration with MBTA fare payment
- Dedicated pick-up/drop-off locations in Kendall
- Real-time tracking and reliability

### Recommendations

**Near-term (2025-2027):** 
- Pursue microtransit pilot with established operator (Via or Spare)
- Require proven technology platform, not new startup
- Secure MassDOT funding
- Partner with MBTA and Massport
- Learn from Bridj failure: Ensure service is frequent, reliable, fast enough to be useful

**Do not:**
- Launch with unproven startup
- Attempt to own/operate vehicles directly
- Rely on technology alone without addressing traffic congestion
- Underfund service leading to infrequent/unreliable operations

## 4. Smart Corridor Technology

### Transit Signal Priority (TSP)

**How TSP Works:**
- Adjusts traffic signal timing for approaching transit vehicles
- Either extends green times or shortens red times
- Makes minor modifications to signal cycle/phasing
- Reduces transit vehicle waiting at red lights

**Documented Benefits:**
- Reduced transit travel times
- Improved schedule adherence
- Increased road network efficiency (person mobility)
- Los Angeles County Metro: 25% travel time improvement, 29% speed increase on one line

### Recent Implementations (2024-2025)

**Denver Region (RTD):**
- Spring 2024: Installed at 9 intersections on Havana St, 14 on E. Colfax Ave, 4 on US-36 in Westminster
- Formed Regional TSP Working Group with DRCOG to standardize principles and technologies
- Summer 2025: Expanding to 5 intersections on SH-119, 3 on Coffman St (Longmont), multiple Boulder sites

**Minnesota/Twin Cities:**
- 2023 Legislature required Metropolitan Council working group for transit advantage efforts
- TSP installed at 163 intersections, many more planned
- Coming years: TSP added to most intersections on METRO B and E lines, select Route 63 intersections
- "Projects bundling elements (TSP + bus lanes + stop spacing + route simplification) realize substantial speed/reliability improvements"

**San Francisco Bay Area:**
- IDEA TSP funds awarded to 5 transit priority projects, fall 2024
- Projects may be completed as early as 2027
- Since 2012: Over $130 million invested in transit priority projects (Transit Sustainability Project/TPI)

**Chicago Region (Pace):**
- Plans to deploy TSP along 9 additional corridors
- Approximately 300 signalized intersections

### Technology Requirements

- Transit vehicle GPS/location tracking
- Communication system (vehicle to signal)
- Signal controller upgrades
- Central management system
- Integration with existing traffic management

### Costs

- Signal equipment upgrade: $15,000-$30,000 per intersection
- Vehicle equipment: $3,000-$5,000 per bus
- Central management system: $100,000-$300,000
- Typical corridor (20 intersections): $500,000-$800,000

### Connected Vehicle (V2X) Technology

**Federal Investment (2024):**
FHWA announced $60 million in grants for "Saving Lives with Connectivity: Accelerating V2X Deployment"

**Arizona ($19.6M):**
- Maricopa County DOT leading large-scale V2X deployment
- 750 physical roadside units + virtual roadside units
- ~400 vehicle onboard units (transit, emergency, freight fleets)
- Primarily 5.9 GHz communications

**Texas ($19.2M):**
- Greater Houston area, College Station, connecting corridors
- V2X deployment near Texas A&M campus

**Utah ($20M):**
- Three-state deployment: Utah, Colorado, Wyoming
- All of Utah, I-80 through Wyoming, major portions of Colorado including Denver Metro

**Wyoming I-80 Corridor:**
- 402 miles, 70% truck traffic
- V2I and V2V connectivity for freight efficiency/safety
- Roadside alerts, parking notifications, dynamic travel guidance
- Phase 4: Replacing DSRC with Cellular V2X (C-V2X) following FCC 2020 ruling

**Global Momentum:**
- China, U.S., Germany leading public-private partnerships for C-V2X
- China and South Korea: Dense 5G coverage enabling large-scale 5G-V2X deployment
- Germany: Federal Ministry promoting connected infrastructure on corridors (A9 Autobahn testbed)

**DOT Plans (September 2024):**
- Advance V2X along highways and intersections
- Improve road safety, traffic efficiency, accessibility
- Deploy connected vehicle technologies in key transportation corridors
- Develop standards for V2X communication protocols

### Application to Kendall-Logan Corridor

**Immediate Benefits from TSP:**
- Silver Line bus already operates on portions of route
- TSP could reduce 3-5 minutes on Kendall-Airport trip
- Relatively low cost for significant impact
- Proven technology, straightforward implementation

**Medium-Term V2X Integration:**
- Could integrate airport shuttles into connected corridor
- Real-time traffic optimization
- Priority routing around congestion
- Communication with airport operations for flight-based demand

### Realistic Timeline for Kendall-Logan

**2025-2026: TSP Implementation (1-2 years)**
- Identify key intersections on shuttle route
- Coordinate with Cambridge, Boston, and MassPort
- Install TSP on 10-15 critical intersections
- Equip shuttle vehicles with TSP technology
- Estimated cost: $300,000-$500,000

**2027-2030: V2X Pilot (3-5 years)**
- Participate in regional V2X corridor development
- Integrate Kendall-Logan as demonstration corridor
- Seek federal V2X deployment funding
- Coordinate with MIT research partnerships
- Estimated cost: $2-3 million (with federal match)

### Recommendations

**High Priority - Immediate Action:**
Pursue TSP implementation for Kendall-Logan corridor. This is the highest ROI technology for near-term implementation:
- Proven technology
- Moderate cost
- Significant time savings
- Can be implemented within 18-24 months
- Foundation for future V2X integration

**Medium Priority - Position for Future:**
Monitor federal V2X funding opportunities and position Kendall-Logan as demonstration corridor, particularly with MIT partnership potential.

## 5. Electric/Sustainable Vehicles

### Industry Developments

**Recent Airport Electric Bus Deployments:**

**Kansas City International Airport (MCI):**
- Transitioning to all-electric buses with wireless charging system
- Ordered 7 additional BYD electric buses (delivered March 2024)
- **First airport in world to use inductive charging for passenger shuttles**
- Two 300 kW wireless inductive chargers at terminal bus stops
- Supports entire parking fleet of ~28 vehicles
- Part of $1.5 billion new terminal project
- Buses charge while positioned over in-concrete pads

**Munich Airport (Germany):**
- Ordered 49 MAN electric buses (option for 25 more)
- First 10 solo vehicles delivered to AeroGround subsidiary
- Range: Up to 350 km
- 40 charging stations from SBRS (Shell group)

**Oakland Airport:**
- Fleet of 5 electric shuttle buses
- 5 Heliox 180kW DC fast chargers
- Funding: $1.5M FAA Zero Emissions Vehicle (ZEV) + $600K California Air Resources Board

### Charging Infrastructure

**Technology Options:**

1. **Plug-in DC Fast Charging:**
   - Most common: 150-180 kW chargers
   - Charge time: 2-4 hours for full charge
   - Cost per charger: $50,000-$100,000

2. **Wireless Inductive Charging (MCI model):**
   - Charge while parked at terminals
   - 300 kW systems
   - No cables/plugs required
   - Higher upfront cost but lower operational complexity
   - Cost per charger: $200,000-$300,000

**Industry Planning Resources:**
- National Academies 2024 guide categorizes charging for 8 use cases including shuttlebus
- Covers: Charging station ownership, load shapes, fees, siting, performance metrics

### Environmental & Cost Benefits

**Operating Cost Savings:**
- Electricity vs. diesel: 60-70% reduction in fuel costs
- Maintenance: 40-50% reduction (fewer moving parts)
- 20-year lifecycle: $400,000-$600,000 savings per bus

**Environmental Benefits:**
- Zero direct emissions
- Noise reduction
- Air quality improvement (especially important in airport/urban environments)
- Marketing value for sustainable airport access

**Capital Costs:**
- Electric bus: $750,000-$850,000 vs. diesel bus: $450,000-$550,000
- Premium: ~$300,000 per bus
- Charging infrastructure: $500,000-$1.5M for small fleet (5-10 buses)

### Funding Opportunities

**Federal:**
- FAA Zero Emissions Vehicle (ZEV) program
- FHWA grants for clean vehicle deployment
- Biden infrastructure bill allocations

**State:**
- MassCEC (Massachusetts Clean Energy Center) programs
- Massport already received MassCEC grant for EV expansion at Logan
- MBTA precedent for electric bus adoption

**Massachusetts Context:**
- Massport + MassCEC partnership for electric vehicles at Logan Airport
- MassCEC ACT4All (Accelerating Clean Transportation for All) program
- Statewide focus on EV infrastructure and reducing emissions in overburdened areas

### Fleet Size Analysis for Kendall-Logan

**Demand Assumptions:**
- Peak frequency: Every 10 minutes
- Operating hours: 5am-12am (19 hours)
- Number of trips per day per bus: ~15-20 round trips
- Fleet requirement: 5-7 buses (including spares)

**Charging Strategy:**
- Opportunity charging at terminals (10-15 min charge between trips)
- Overnight depot charging
- Mix of 3-4 fast chargers at Kendall and Logan

**2025-2026: Immediate Implementation Possible**
- Electric bus technology mature and proven
- Multiple vendors (BYD, Proterra, New Flyer, others)

   - Estimated cost: $5-6 million (5 buses + charging infrastructure)

2. **Year 2 (2026):**

Electric shuttle buses should be the foundation of any Kendall-Logan service:

1. **Apply for FAA ZEV funding in 2025** - Oakland precedent shows $1.5M available
2. **Partner with MassCEC** - Massport precedent shows state support for airport EV

## 6. Mobility-as-a-Service (MaaS) Integration

### MaaS Definition & Concept

Mobility as a Service integrates various transportation modes into a single accessible on-demand service through a unified digital platform. Users plan, book, and pay for multiple transport types through one app/account.

**Core Features:**
- Single app for all transportation modes
- Integrated journey planning
- Unified payment/ticketing
- Real-time information
- Subscription or pay-per-use options

### Industry Examples

**Helsinki (Whim):**
- Most advanced MaaS implementation
- Goal: Make private car ownership unnecessary by 2025
- Integrates public transit, taxis, bikes, car-sharing
- Subscription plans ($60-600/month)

**Cities Trialing MaaS (2024-2025):**
- Paris, Eindhoven, Gothenburg, Montpellier, Vienna, Hanover (Europe)
- Las Vegas, Los Angeles, Denver (USA)
- Singapore, Barcelona (Global)

**Athens Demo (2022):**
- Travel Companion application
- Public transport operator + bike-sharing + taxi + municipal transit
- Journey planning and integrated ticketing through single app

**Cubic Transportation Systems + SkedGo:**
- Partnership to enhance MaaS offerings
- Unified platform for planning, booking, paying across multimodal journeys
- Integrates public transit, ride-sharing, biking, other modes

### Airport Integration

**Landline Example:**
- Building infrastructure for bags, security, integration into airline booking
- Enables buses, trains, and future flying cars to be sold as flight connections
- "Integration of local, regional, and national infrastructures"
- Duplicating airport services (bag check, security) in city centers

**Key Integration Requirements:**
- Airline booking system integration
- Baggage handling coordination
- Security clearance processes
- Real-time flight information
- Seamless ticketing across modes

### Technology Components

**Account-Based Ticketing:**
- Acts as unified mobility account
- Passengers tap across journey without pre-purchasing tickets
- Charged appropriate fare for actual use
- Eliminates need for mode-specific tickets/passes

**Platform Requirements:**
- Journey planning algorithms
- Real-time data feeds from all operators
- Payment processing/settlement
- Customer account management
- Mobile app + web interface

### Implementation Challenges

**From ITF-OECD Roundtable:**
- Integrating public transport into MaaS requires careful planning
- Public transit agencies concerned about revenue loss, customer data, service quality control
- Need for standardized data formats and APIs
- Governance questions: Who owns customer relationship?

**Key Success Factors:**
- Strong public transit agency participation/leadership
- Open data standards
- Regulatory framework supporting integration
- Public subsidy/support for platform development
- Focus on user needs, not technology for technology's sake

### Application to Kendall-Logan

**Near-Term Integration:**
1. **Add Kendall-Logan shuttle to existing apps:**
   - MBTA mTicket/CharlieCard app
   - Transit app
   - Google Maps integration
   - Citymapper (if available in Boston)

2. **Account-based payment:**
   - Accept CharlieCard
   - Accept credit card tap
   - Accept Apple Pay/Google Pay
   - Link with MBTA fare structure (transfer credit)

3. **Real-time information:**
   - Live vehicle locations
   - Arrival predictions
   - Integration with flight tracking
   - Crowding information

**Medium-Term MaaS Platform:**
1. **Single ticket for multi-modal trips:**
   - MBTA subway + Kendall shuttle + airline
   - Price as through-ticket with transfer discount
   - Enable booking days in advance

2. **Journey planning integration:**
   - "Cambridge to LAX" shows: Red Line + Shuttle + Flight
   - Compare with: TNC to airport, parking options
   - Show total door-to-door time and cost

3. **Airline integration:**
   - Appear in airline booking as ground transport option
   - Coordinate with flight times
   - Baggage handling consideration (if feasible)

- GTFS data feed for real-time information

## 8. Summary of Realistic Timelines

### Immediate Implementation (0-2 years, 2025-2027)

**High Feasibility:**
1. **Electric Shuttle Buses:** Mature technology, multiple vendors, funding available
2. **Transit Signal Priority (TSP):** Proven technology, moderate cost, high ROI
3. **Basic MaaS Integration:** GTFS feed, map apps, CharlieCard acceptance
4. **Microtransit Pilot:** Partner with Via/Spare, limited hours initially

**Estimated Cost:** $6-8 million total
- Electric shuttle + TSP: $6-7M
- MaaS integration: $200-300K
- OR Microtransit instead: $800K-1.2M

**Funding Viability:** Strong
- Multiple federal grant programs (FAA ZEV)
- State support (MassCEC, MassDOT)
- Local partnership (Massport, Cambridge, MIT)

### Near-Term Expansion (2-5 years, 2027-2030)

**Medium Feasibility:**
1. **Enhanced MaaS Platform:** Through-ticketing, multi-modal journey planning
2. **V2X Corridor Pilot:** Connected vehicle technology, participate in regional deployment
3. **Expanded Microtransit:** 24/7 service, broader Kendall coverage
4. **Autonomous Shuttle Pilot (Late Stage):** If regulations advance, small-scale test

**Estimated Cost:** $3-5 million
- MaaS platform development: $500K-1M
- V2X corridor participation: $2-3M (with federal match)
- Microtransit expansion: $500K-1M/year

**Funding Viability:** Moderate
- Dependent on success of initial pilot
- Federal V2X grants competitive
- State/local funding requires demonstrated demand

### Medium-Term Possibilities (5-10 years, 2030-2035)

**Uncertain Feasibility:**
1. **Autonomous Shuttle Service:** Regulatory framework mature, technology costs down
2. **Full V2X Integration:** Connected corridor operational
3. **Regional MaaS Network:** Kendall-Logan part of broader system
4. **Expanded Electric Fleet:** Additional routes, higher frequency

**Estimated Cost:** $10-15 million
- Autonomous shuttle deployment: $5-8M
- V2X full deployment: $3-5M
- Regional MaaS: $2-3M (Kendall-Logan portion)

**Funding Viability:** Uncertain
- Dependent on federal/state policy priorities
- Technology cost trajectories
- Ridership and revenue from earlier phases

### Long-Term Vision (10+ years, 2035+)

**Speculative:**
1. **eVTOL Integration:** Only if Boston develops vertiport infrastructure and economics improve
2. **Fully Autonomous Network:** No safety attendants, complete optimization
3. **Comprehensive Regional MaaS:** Seamless integration across all modes, all destinations

**Not Recommended for Planning Purposes:**
- Too many unknowns in technology, regulation, economics
- Focus resources on near and medium-term deployments

## 9. Key Recommendations

### DO Pursue (High Priority):

1. **Electric Shuttle with Transit Signal Priority**
   - Immediate action: Begin planning in 2025
   - Target launch: 2026-2027
   - Budget: $6-7M
   - This should be the foundation of Kendall-Logan mobility

2. **Basic MaaS Integration**
   - Parallel to shuttle deployment
   - CharlieCard acceptance, GTFS feed, app integration
   - Budget: $200-300K
   - Enhances shuttle utility and convenience

3. **Partnership Development**
   - MIT research collaboration
   - Massport operational partnership
   - MBTA fare integration
   - Technology vendor partnerships

4. **Grant Applications**
   - FAA ZEV program
   - MassCEC ACT4All
   - MassDOT discretionary funding
   - Begin applications in 2025

### CONSIDER Pursuing (Medium Priority):

1. **Microtransit Pilot**
   - Alternative OR complement to fixed-route shuttle
   - Partner with Via or Spare (proven operators)
   - Budget: $800K-1.2M for 1-year pilot
   - Learn from Bridj failure: Ensure adequate funding, frequency, reliability

2. **V2X Corridor Participation**
   - Medium-term (2027-2030)
   - Monitor federal funding opportunities
   - Position Kendall-Logan as demonstration corridor
   - Leverage MIT partnership

3. **Autonomous Shuttle Pilot**
   - Later phase (2028-2030) if regulations advance
   - Small-scale test, not full deployment
   - Monitor MassDOT regulatory developments
   - Budget: $300-500K for limited pilot

### DO NOT Pursue:

1. **eVTOL for Kendall-Logan**
   - Route too short
   - No infrastructure
   - Economics unfavorable
   - No operator interest
   - Timeline too distant (2035+)

2. **Unproven Microtransit Startups**
   - Bridj lesson: Capital-intensive private models fail
   - Stick with Via, Spare, or similar proven operators
   - Require public subsidy and partnership

3. **Technology for Technology's Sake**
   - Don't pursue AV just because it's "innovative"
   - Don't underfund service to deploy more technology
   - Focus on user needs: frequency, reliability, speed, convenience
